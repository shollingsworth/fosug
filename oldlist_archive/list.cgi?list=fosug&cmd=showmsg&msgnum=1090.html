> Well, if there's one word of advice when it comes to considering

>
> The big thing I like about DD-WRT is that you can quickly set up either
> on-device storage (internal flash; beware of wearing out your flash
> chips!), on-device add-on storage (think:  USB storage, like MMC/SD, CF,
> etc), or off-device network storage (SMB, NFS, sshfs).  This will allow
> you to install more and bigger packages and in ways that doesn't kill
> your router's on-board flash memory.
>
> Apparently, even some NAS devices have many of the same chipsets,
> notably from BroadCom.  I see making a smallish Squid server to replace
> my K6 machine which sucks up a LOT more juice.  What'd be even scarier
> is making a smallish "data center" where your big ethernet switch sucks
> up more juice than all your other connected appliances combined!
>
> And I like OpenWRT for its package management system (which can be used
> on DD-WRT as well, though I suspect a lot of the kernel-bound stuff
> might prove to be a challenge both installing and using).  It's
> essentially an embedded system's answer to APT.
>
> In any event, if you can't find a good WRT54G or GS or can't justify
> buying a WRT54GL (which is specifically targeted to folks like us), the
> Buffalo is a great alternative.  There are other routers that will take
> this firmware as well.
>
> The basic message is this:  It's amazing what you can get for $50 or so.
>   Once you flash these guys, you get enterprise-class features for a
> significantly small fraction of the cost of getting enterprise-class gear.
>
> --Ian.
