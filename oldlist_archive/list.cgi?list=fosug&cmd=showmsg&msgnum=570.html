I'm going to reply here to this message instead of the other ones. SBCIS has
been blocking outgoing SMTP (TCP/UDP Port 25) for several months. I used to
send such small amounts of mail I didn't know about this for a few months
until I looked at my logs...Yeah, I know Bad Admin, no cookie for you.

Anyways, this is completely Opt-Outable as noted in the SBC message you posted
earlier. You just need to configure your Postfix (or other MTA) to relay
outgoing mail through SBC's SMTP servers. It's a one-line fix. Of course,
depending on how old your account is (mine dates from pre-sbcglobal.net
####@####.#### account...)) and the info on their site is incorrect for some
people (like me.)

Basically they are trying to stop the email worms that go out and ravage the
Internet via SMTP. That should work, until a worm can actively search in IE/OE
for the "Outgoing SMTP server". At which time, something new will need to be
tried. Probably on to using authentication for relaying. I know my ISP has
this enabled.

Also, this fixes the issue with some mail hosts not accepting mail directly
from dymanic IP addresses, cable ISP IPs, etc., ala AOL. I think SBC gladly
replays all mail sent to their SMTP servers via their own networks, no matter
the content. They don't care what the FROM or REPLY addresses are. I still
receive mail on 25 w/o problems (as well as 80, 22, etc.).

As far as the government, specifically the FTC, RECOMMENDED that ISPs block
outgoing port 25 and direct users to specifically using the ISP's outgoing
SMTP servers. This was not a rule that the ISP must do this, just a measure
they could take.

Here's a Slashdot article about this, however, the linked CNN.COM article is
gone:
http://it.slashdot.org/comments.pl?sid=150605&threshold=1&mode=nested&commentsort=0&op=Change

As for actually stopping spam, hard to say. The spammer can just send through
their ISPs SMTP server. Though, I'm sure it would be noticed quickly and said
user would probably be banned. Not that that would ever stop them.
