Note, to make a flash or jump, or thumb drive usable in linux. You
need 3 things compiled in your kernel. Either monolithic (all in 1
kernel) or in moduals.

USB Support
USB Mass storage
and
(whatever Filesystem your card is) In 99% of cases it's Fat16 (so you
would use vfat)

Once thoes are compiled and running, simply plug in device.  if you
check the dmesg log you will see it showup as a Scsi drive.  /dev/sda
for example. then you mount it like any normal drive

mount /dev/sda1 /mnt/flash
or whatever they happen to be.


Second note. ALL USB Flash/Pen/Thumb/Jump/Toe drives are linux
compatable (unless they are a crappy knock off, and I havn't seen a
non-standard knock off in about 7 years).  Easiest way to verify if
you will have problems with your drive.  PLug it into any windows ME
or XP computer and if It detects without the need for drivers, it is a
standard MASS STORAGE USB device.  Virtualy every usb storage device
uses this, harddrives, flash, most digital cameras. etc

This is NOT a thumb drive(at least not in the traditional sense). Most
people consider a thumb drive to have a USB interface(which this one
has) with flash memory(this one is a hard drive). This is more like a
external hard drive which gets it's power from USB(not really a good
idea for an external hard drive, imho).

If you want an external USB hard drive like this, you could build it
yourself (external USB hard drive enclosure($30) + 80 GB hd ($50)) for
much cheaper.

If the device is a laptop harddrive or even better yet a micro drive.
The power drain is minimal to run them.  I would not worry about
running them off a usb.  They will significantly reduce the battery
life on a laptop tho.  3.5 (full size) harddrives can ACTUALLY be
powered off usb. however they push it to it's limit.  Cheap hardware
might burnout.  So you will RARELY see a full size harddrive run off
usb. IF you have one. Get the external adaptor. or invest in a new
box. ($20-30 for nice external case).
