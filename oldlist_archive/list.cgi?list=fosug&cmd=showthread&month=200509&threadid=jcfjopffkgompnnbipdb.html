I feel your pain.  I'm a microsoft certified tech
(please no tomatoes) and I've scene similar instances
of stupid stuff like that happening.  I hate to see
developer and/or software companies develop anything
with microsoft syndrome (ie the compulsive need to
make your decisions for you without giving you any
choices and/or options).

Some of my fellow co-workers where I work (I work for
a VISP) ask me why I have anti-windows desktops and I
work on a windows XP machine. I know this is more a
dev thing than windows in general but it's gotten to
the point where it's synonamous to me.  For me it's
not that I hate the company any more than anybody else
its more about I hate the mindset used on how they
create their software that has spread to other
companies, "If microsoft can get away with it so can
I." That in turn drives down the quality of
production.  

Hence my learning Unix based OS's.  The open source
testing model I think is far superior to the
traditional testing modem.

I was installing a printer on a network (not a network
printer mind you) , but oh wait for some reason the
server the printer is attached to is taking up WAY too
much processor time when anybody prints and other
services on the server become stalled (or occasionally
lock up and/or crash then don't respond anymore even
when the process is done).  Turns out the driver
manufacturer made the driver so that it runs at a
higher priority level than normal(and it was probably
made poorly anyway) that also ate up gobs of ram. I
tend to think of it now as the Godzilla print driver. 

A fix came out later in the form of better drivers
thankfully.

I feel your pain drew.  That is one of a few reasons
why I don't do many side jobs anymore.

Ok.  On a more serious note (everyone gets that the last post was a funny, I
hope), you should *never* do something like this on a production system.  That
they required you to do such a thing is just bad (on the part of your customer
and your boss that told you to do it).  

I believe you would have been money ahead to simply purchase[1] a USB jet
direct[2] and make the printer network aware via that interface.  This pushes
the driver installs out to the clients hopefully and eliminates adding
complexity to your server.

The list prices on these guys is usually somewhere in the $300 or less range.
The downtime the bean counters had was most assuredly worth more that this
(and this is the angle to take with them if they resist purchasing the items).

HTH,
