On Wed, Oct 12, 2005 at 03:23:19PM -0700, Robert Nickel wrote:
> 
> Yes it does save a few items.  First, there is no asking of you to give your
> existing property over to another's control.  Under the GPL, you can still
> hold copyright to your work and at a later date remove new development from
> the public eye (Nessus is doing this right now as a matter of fact).

I agree with Robert in that the GPL isn't communism, as it isn't required. You
don't HAVE to use the GPL.

> The assumption being made here is that communism is, in and of itself, a bad
> thing.  The examples had to date were bad because they did not create the
> idealistic goal of what communism "stands for" for the people affected.  Quite
> the contrary actually.  It has created a two caste society which rewards the
> status-quo and punishes anyone who shows initiative.

I think we need to differentiate communism and totalitarianism. Communism is
an economic model, as is a free market. Totalitarianism is a political form of
government ala democracy, republics, monarchies, etc. A pure form of communism
is probably not attainable, as is a free market. Greed takes over at some
point.

So far, all of the "communist governments" have actually been totalitarian
governments hiding behind a mask of communism. You don't see the leaders, or
their children starving or freezing to death. China is interesting as of late,
that while the governement is still totalitarian, it is allowing some free
market to exist, but in a controlled manner.

Pure democracies are also very hard to do. So in the US we have a
representative democracy. We vote on people to vote they way we would.

> Do I think communism as a political scheme is a bad idea?  Absolutely.

Remember, communism is an economic model. Not a political model.

> Do I think communism as a philosophy is morally flawed?  Not so sure.  I've
> been around long enough to see the merits of giving up what you don't need for
> those who may have less.  This is referred to as charity when it's voluntary
> an communism when it's not.  The difference is in the practice.  This is where
> the GPL sits and it walks a fine line.  Saying that software *should* be free
> is somewhat risque as it infers that you should be required to make your
> software free (communism-like).  However, the GPL requires action on the part
> of the  copyright holder to either give over his copyright to the FSF or to
> invoke the GPL on his code (charity like).
> 
> It's an interesting debate.  One best had in a social setting so that you can
> see the red faces of the folks who are philosophically and emotionally
> attached to the issue.

That's true.

> > Maybe I'm playing the devils advocate by saying that. Because the

> > on it.
> 
> Nah.  Nothing wrong with asking.  It's a good way to start understanding what
> you may be "voting for" with your usage patterns and advocacy.

> --Robert
