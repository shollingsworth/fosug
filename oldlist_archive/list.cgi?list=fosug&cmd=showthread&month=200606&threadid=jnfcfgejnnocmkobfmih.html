You know that OpenOffice.org Impress can export your presentations or 
power points as a flash movie (.swf file).
Also, you can use VNCRec to record your presentation directly from your 
desktop.  (VNCRec http://www.sodan.org/~penny/vncrec).  I also use 
vnc2swf at work to create training presentations.
(vnc2swf http://www.unixuser.org/~euske/vnc2swf/)

With vnc2swf, you can import audio into it as well with command line 
tools.  Using Audacity you can capture the audio of the presentation and 
later dub it into the flash video.

Here's a good write up http://linuxgazette.net/102/washko.html

The advantages of vnc2swf are:

1. the flash files are much smaller, so hosting them on a web server  
is easier and it's not such a huge download for the poor guy with a  
slow connection.
2.  It's cross platform compatable, so for instance if you were  
showing of a really cool FOSS app that is written for window$ you can  
do that too.

Or, as another example, at work, my important servers are all Linux  
based, but my desktop is Windows XP.  vnc2swf runs on Windows, you  
just need to install Python and the two other dependancies.  This has  
been very valuable to me to help make video tutorials for my end  
users, they are a special bunch and it's saved me a lot of time trying  
to explain really simple stuff to them.

Last night, I did just a really basic example, I didn't think to  
resize the video output size, but you can make it so it's not so huge,  
or leave it huge, it's easier to view text that way.

Now, if you were going to make DVDs of the FOSUG presentations, and  
sell them for a few bucks to raise money for the group, then yeah, AVI  
files would be the way to go.

For the most part though, this would probably really help to improve  
the videos from the meetings.  I've downloaded several to see what  
I've been missing (and also because the local group here really  
sucks...) and it's often times very difficult to see what's going on  
on the screens.  The camera doesn't pick pick up the smaller text when  
it's projected.

If you did go with the AVI file though, you could start using your  
video editing skills, by having video of the presentor and then  
cutting to the VNC output to show the screens when they are talking  
about certain examples... that would be pretty cool.
