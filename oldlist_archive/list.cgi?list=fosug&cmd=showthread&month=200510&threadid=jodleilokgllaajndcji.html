Hi, all.

Just installed one machine, Kurumi, and another which was actually a 
reinstall, Yuushi, with Ubuntu Breezy.  Both are multimedia boxen, 
specifically, anime screening boxes.

Everything went very smoothly once I realized I had to enable universe 
and multiverse to get things working the way I needed.  Adding 
ftp.nerim.net and I was REALLY good to go (so I can play DVDs and 
anything requiring a Windows DLL to work).  Also added Kubuntu because I 
prefer KDE over GNOME.

The one thing that struck me is that IEEE 1394 and USB 2.0 storage 
devices worked with minimal fuss.  In fact, the bonus I gained was that 
I don't have to worry about mounting the device I plug in; it appears on 
my desktop.  Adding files from those devices is also a snap since any 
devices plugged in have their own "folder" in the standard KDE dialog.

The only thing I don't like is Ubuntu's heavy reliance on sudo.  I 
prefer an actual root prompt personally.  And since I have further 
issues with sudo, I'm not so sure I want Ubuntu running as a server OS. 
  However, for a desktop and a non-mission-critical infrastructure 
machine, it's wonderful.

Overall, Ubuntu looks like a great desktop OS.  For multimedia, once you 
enable and add the appropriate repositories, it's as good as or better 
than Debian.  Further, the KDE build that you can get is rock-solid, 
though Kaffeine, my player of choice, has a few bugs, but that seems to 
be more an upstream problem than anything else.  It has issues in Debian 
as well.

Those're my experiences.  Hope this helps people along!

--Ian.

On the server side, I am very uncomfortable with its security history. 
If I have to assign someone access to the machine even if for a specific 
task, I'd feel far more comfortable if it were someone I could trust 
with full root access.  Granted, there are many other setuid programs 
they could exploit, but this one, by far, has me the most alarmed.

Put simply, sudo strikes me as a potential security nightmare when it 
comes to code audits.  su, on the other hand, is far simpler.  At that 
point, the onus of delegating privileges on the system rests squarely on 
the shoulders of the admin, where it truly belongs.  I can understand 
why some people would use it.  I personally am not one of those people. 
  To me, when it comes to privlege access at the command prompt level, 
it's all or nothing.

On the desktop side, I suppose I could live with it, but that still 
doesn't change my feelings overall toward sudo.

Yes it does save a few items.  First, there is no asking of you to give your
existing property over to another's control.  Under the GPL, you can still
hold copyright to your work and at a later date remove new development from
the public eye (Nessus is doing this right now as a matter of fact).

> Don't get me wrong, I'm all about contributing to the better good,

The assumption being made here is that communism is, in and of itself, a bad
thing.  The examples had to date were bad because they did not create the
idealistic goal of what communism "stands for" for the people affected.  Quite
the contrary actually.  It has created a two caste society which rewards the
status-quo and punishes anyone who shows initiative.

The bad of communism isn't that the ideal is bad, it's that the implementation
is impossible unless *all* of the parties involved agree to be bound by the
system.  This has proven to be an impossibility for application on the scale
of nations as most the folks looking to implement "the party" for government
are interested in the power and ses communism as a great way to sway idealists
to their side.

Do I think communism as a political scheme is a bad idea?  Absolutely.
Do I think communism as a philosophy is morally flawed?  Not so sure.  I've
been around long enough to see the merits of giving up what you don't need for
those who may have less.  This is referred to as charity when it's voluntary
an communism when it's not.  The difference is in the practice.  This is where
the GPL sits and it walks a fine line.  Saying that software *should* be free
is somewhat risque as it infers that you should be required to make your
software free (communism-like).  However, the GPL requires action on the part
of the  copyright holder to either give over his copyright to the FSF or to
invoke the GPL on his code (charity like).

It's an interesting debate.  One best had in a social setting so that you can
see the red faces of the folks who are philosophically and emotionally
attached to the issue.
